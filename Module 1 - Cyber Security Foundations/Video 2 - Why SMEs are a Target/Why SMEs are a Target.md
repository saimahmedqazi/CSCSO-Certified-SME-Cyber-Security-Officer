## [[Constrained Budget]]

Limited financial resources often mean that cybersecurity is treated as a luxury rather than a necessity, leading to "brittle" defenses that are easily shattered.

## [[Insufficient Cyber Defenses]]

SMEs often rely on basic, consumer-grade security tools that are unable to detect or stop modern, multi-stage attacks.

## [[Neglected Software]]

Because of a lack of time or compatibility fears, many SMEs run applications and systems that are missing critical security patches.

## [[Limited IT Expertise and Resources]]

Without a dedicated security team, the responsibility often falls on generalists who may not have the specialized training to hunt for hidden threats.

## [[Limited Governance]]

The absence of formal policies and oversight means that security is often reactive and "accidental" rather than proactive and planned.

## [[Inadequate Training]]

Staff members who aren't trained to spot digital red flags become unintentional "open doors" for attackers through simple human error.

## [[Direct Access to Decision Makers]]

The flat structure of SMEs makes it easier for hackers to impersonate owners or CEOs and trick employees into bypassing security protocols.

## [[Substantial Data Reservoirs]]

Small firms often hold massive amounts of sensitive PII (Personally Identifiable Information) that is highly valuable on the dark web.

## [[Dependence on Third Parties]]

Relying on external vendors for critical services creates a "weakest link" problem where a vendor's breach becomes the SME's breach.

## [[Supply Chain Gateway]]

Attackers target SMEs not for their own data, but as a trusted "stepping stone" to reach the more hardened networks of their larger enterprise clients.
